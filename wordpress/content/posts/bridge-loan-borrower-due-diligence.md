# The Complete Guide to Bridge Loan Borrower Due Diligence

Every bridge lender has a story about the borrower who looked great on paper. Good credit. Reasonable LTV. Solid project plan. And then things fell apart — because nobody checked the entity, verified the track record, or searched for prior litigation.

Borrower due diligence is the highest-risk gap in bridge lending. Most lenders still do it manually, inconsistently, or not at all. This guide covers the four pillars of borrower validation and how to build a repeatable process that catches problems before they become losses.

---

## The Four Pillars of Borrower Validation

Every borrower validation should cover these four areas. Skip one and you're exposed.

### 1. Entity Validation

The borrowing entity is the legal counterparty to your loan. If that entity is suspended, dissolved, or doesn't exist, you have a problem.

**What to check:**
- **SOS status** — Is the entity active and in good standing? Every state maintains a Secretary of State database. An entity that's suspended for unpaid franchise taxes or dissolved for failure to file annual reports cannot legally transact business.
- **Formation date** — How old is the entity? A brand-new LLC (formed within the last 6 months) applying for a large bridge loan deserves extra scrutiny.
- **Registered agent** — Is the registered agent still active? A resigned agent often indicates neglect or abandonment.
- **Foreign registration** — If the entity is formed in one state but the property is in another, verify the entity is registered to do business in the property state.

**Red flags:**
- Entity formed within 90 days of the loan application
- Suspended or not in good standing
- Registered agent has resigned
- Entity name on application doesn't match SOS records exactly

The manual version of this check involves visiting up to 50 different state SOS websites, each with a different interface and search process. It takes 5-15 minutes per state, per entity.

### 2. Track Record Verification

Borrowers self-report their track records. They tell you they've done 20 flips, made money on all of them, and never had a problem. The question is: can you verify any of that?

**What to check:**
- **Property records** — Search property records by the borrower's name and entity name. How many acquisitions and dispositions show up? Do they match what the borrower claims?
- **Project types** — What kinds of projects has the borrower done? Flips, ground-up construction, buy-and-hold? Match this to what they're applying to do now.
- **Outcomes** — Of the completed projects, how many were profitable? How many are still held with no activity? How many ended in distress?
- **Experience tier** — Based on verified completions, where does this borrower fall? A borrower with 10+ verified completions is a fundamentally different risk than someone with zero.

**Red flags:**
- Borrower claims 20 projects but property records show 5
- Multiple properties still held years after acquisition with no disposition
- Prior projects in a completely different market or project type
- No track record under the borrowing entity (all under a different entity or personal name)

### 3. Litigation Screening

Prior litigation tells you how a borrower handles disputes, obligations, and failure. It's not that every lawsuit is disqualifying — it's that undisclosed litigation is a pattern indicator.

**What to check:**
- **Federal bankruptcy (PACER)** — Has the borrower or entity filed for bankruptcy? Chapter 7 (liquidation) and Chapter 11 (reorganization) are both relevant. Even dismissed cases tell you something.
- **Foreclosures** — Has the borrower lost properties to foreclosure? One foreclosure in a 20-year career may be explainable. Three foreclosures in three years is a pattern.
- **Active lawsuits** — Are there pending lawsuits against the borrower? Construction defect claims, lender lawsuits, partner disputes?
- **Lis pendens** — Are there lis pendens (pending litigation notices) recorded against any of the borrower's properties? These cloud title and threaten your exit.

**Red flags:**
- Undisclosed bankruptcy (even if discharged)
- Active lis pendens on properties currently owned
- Pattern of construction defect lawsuits
- Multiple foreclosures

### 4. GC Credential Validation

For construction bridge loans (rehabs, ground-up, value-add), the general contractor is as important as the borrower. An unlicensed, uninsured, or disciplined GC puts the entire project budget at risk.

**What to check:**
- **License status** — Is the GC's license active and current in the state where the work will be done? Different states have different licensing requirements.
- **License classification** — Does the license type match the work? A residential handyman license doesn't cover a $500K commercial rehab.
- **Disciplinary history** — Has the licensing board taken action against this contractor? Citations, fines, and suspensions are public record.
- **Insurance** — Does the GC carry general liability and workers' compensation insurance? Are the policies current?

**Red flags:**
- Expired or suspended license
- License type doesn't match the scope of work
- Active disciplinary actions
- No workers' comp coverage
- GC is a related party to the borrower (ownership overlap)

## Building a Repeatable Process

The biggest risk in borrower due diligence isn't missing a single check — it's inconsistency. When each analyst on your team does checks differently, things get missed.

**A repeatable process means:**

1. **Every borrower gets the same checks.** No exceptions for "I know this guy" or "they've borrowed from us before." Conditions change. Entities lapse. New lawsuits get filed.

2. **Results are documented.** When your investor or auditor asks what due diligence you performed, you can show them. Every check type, every data source, every flag.

3. **Flags are actionable.** A suspended entity isn't an automatic decline — but it requires a conversation and a documented decision. A flag with no follow-up is worse than no flag at all.

4. **The process is fast enough to not skip.** If due diligence takes 4 hours per borrower, people will skip it when they're busy. If it takes 2 minutes, it becomes part of every deal.

## The Cost of Getting It Wrong

Bridge loan defaults don't just cost you the interest you would have earned. They cost:

- **Legal fees** — Foreclosure, eviction, litigation defense
- **Carrying costs** — Insurance, taxes, maintenance on a property you didn't plan to own
- **Opportunity cost** — Capital tied up in a non-performing asset instead of earning returns
- **Reputation damage** — With your investors, your warehouse lender, and in the market

Most of these costs are avoidable with proper borrower validation. The $35-50 cost of a comprehensive validation is trivial compared to the six-figure cost of a single bad loan.
